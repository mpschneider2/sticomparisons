Below, the difference between "treatable" and "curable" is that "curable" means that the infection can completely go away with medicine. On the other hand, "treatable" means that only the symptoms can be taken care of, and nothing can be done about the underlying infection. Some STIs are not curable with medicine, but for a portion of the population, they do go away by themselves.

Chlamydia Bacterial 1,579,885 U.S. cases (2020) Often none
Chlamydia is the most commonly reported STI in the United States, with over 1.5 million cases being reported in 2020. The most common age group is 14-24 years old. However, it's easily curable with antibiotics. It often doesn't exhibit symptoms, which is why STI testing is important. The few times there are symptoms, they are things like: a burning sensation while peeing, and abnormal discharge from the penis/vagina. Gone untreated, it can have negative long-term consequences, such as permanent damage to the female reproductive system. It is passed through the exchange of bodily fluids like semen, pre-ejaculate, and vaginal fluids. Thus, it is important to use barrier methods like condoms and dental dams any time one engages in oral, vaginal, or anal sex. It isn't spread through casual contact so you CAN'T get it from sharing food/drinks, kissing, hugging, holding hands, coughing, sneezing, or sitting on the toilet.

Genital warts Viral 360,000 cases each year None or bumps on genitals/anus
Genital warts are an infection caused by some types of HPV (human papillomavirus). They do NOT cause cancer if left untreated, unlike other types of HPV. They are spread through skin-to-skin contact with someone who’s infected — often during vaginal, anal, and oral sex — and can be spread even when there are no symptoms. Condoms and dental dams can help prevent infection. If there are visible warts, however, condoms and dental dams will not provide reliable protection — they might not cover all the warts. Symptoms may never appear, or they may appear between a few weeks to a few years after sexual contact with an infected person, so it can be difficult to track when and from whom you got the infection. If they do appear, genital warts can show up as small whitish or skin-colored bumps on the genitals or anus.

Genital warts can be treated by removing them, and there is also an HPV vaccine that can prevent most types of genital warts.

Gonorrhea Bacterial 677,769 U.S. cases (2020) Often none
Gonorrhea is a bacterial infection that is common for teens and people in their 20s. Gone untreated, it can lead to serious health problems like infertility. It often doesn’t exhibit symptoms, but, luckily, It's easily curable with antibiotics once one knows they do have it, which is why STI testing is important. The few times there are symptoms, they are things like: a burning sensation while peeing, abnormal discharge from the penis/vagina, and bleeding between periods. It is passed through the exchange of bodily fluids like semen, pre-ejaculate, and vaginal fluids. Thus, it is important to use barrier methods like condoms and dental dams any time one engages in oral, vaginal, or anal sex. It isn't spread through casual contact so you CAN'T get it from sharing food/drinks, kissing, hugging, holding hands, coughing, sneezing, or sitting on the toilet.

Hepatitis B Viral

850,000 U.S. cases (estimate)

 Often none
Prevalence: Estimated 850,000 people in the U.S. are living with hepatitis B, but only 25% of infected individuals are diagnosed.

How it's spread: Hepatitis B is really contagious-- it is transmitted through contact with semen, vaginal fluids, and blood, meaning that one can get it through vaginal, anal, or oral sex, sharing toothbrushes and razors, sharing needles for drugs, piercings, or tattoos, as well as getting it from their parent during birth. You CAN'T get it through sharing food/drinks, kissing, hugging, holding hands, coughing, sneezing, or breastfeeding.

Description: Hepatitis B is an infection that can cause liver disease. Of hepatitis A, B, and C, it is the most common to be spread through sex. To protect yourself, you can get the hepatitis B vaccine and use condoms. There is no cure for hepatitis B, but 90% of adults who get hepatitis B make a full recovery (acute infection). However, about 1 in 20 adults who get hepatitis B become carriers, meaning they have a chronic infection and may continue to spread hepatitis B for the rest of their lives. Because of the toll it takes on your liver, about 1 in 5 people with chronic hepatitis B die from it. There are medicines you can take to treat chronic hepatitis, so talk with your doctor. While most of the time, there aren’t symptoms, common ones include: tiredness, belly pain, loss of appetite, nausea and vomiting, joint pain, headache, fever, hives, etc. When in doubt, get tested!

Herpes Viral

1 in 2 to 1 in 6 people

Often none
While these symptoms are common, most of the time, there are no symptoms, meaning people don't know that they have it. It's a very common infection that stays in your body for life, so more than half of Americans have oral herpes and about 1 in 6 Americans has genital herpes. It is spread through skin-to-skin contact with infected areas, often during vaginal, oral, and anal sex, as well as kissing. Unfortunately, this also means that a pregnant person can pass genital herpes to a baby during vaginal childbirth, but that's rare. Herpes is most contagious when sores are open and wet because the blister fluid easily spreads the virus (but it is still contagious even when one doesn't have sores). Using condoms can help reduce the risk of giving or getting herpes. You can't get herpes from hugging, holding hands, coughing, sneezing, or sitting on toilet seats, as the virus dies quickly outside the body. While it can’t be cured, it can certainly be treated and managed. Besides herpes medicine, treatments for outbreaks include the generic pain-relief methods such as: taking a warm bath, keeping the genital area dry, wearing soft, loose clothes, using an icepack on sores, and taking aspirin/acetaminophen/ibuprofen.

HIV Viral
38,000+ new infections every year Flu-like symptoms right after infection
HIV (human immunodeficiency virus) is a virus that breaks down cells in your immune system, making it harder for your body to fight off diseases that normally aren’t a problem. As a result, infections that wouldn’t usually be a concern can instead make you really sick or even kill you.

How it’s spread: HIV is carried in semen, vaginal fluids, anal mucus, blood, and breast milk. It can be spread through vaginal or anal sex, contact with needles or syringes with HIV-infected blood, and any other way HIV-infected blood, semen, or vaginal fluids could enter open cuts or sores on your body. HIV is NOT spread through saliva or casual contact, and you also can’t get HIV from a toilet seat.

Symptoms: Apart from flu-like symptoms (fever, aches, feeling sick) 2-4 weeks after infection, HIV often doesn’t have symptoms. Once it progresses to the later stage of HIV (AIDS), symptoms can include sore throat, fever, chills, rash, night sweats, muscle aches, fatigue, swollen lymph nodes, and mouth ulcers.

Treatment and prevention: Treatment of HIV is a combination of medicines called antiretroviral therapy that slows down the effects of HIV and can also lower (or even stop) your chances of spreading it. PEP is a series of pills that use ART medications to lower your chances of getting HIV if you take it very soon after you’ve been exposed (within 72 hours, or 3 days). Ways to prevent HIV include taking PrEP or avoiding “high risk” sexual activities.

What is AIDS? How is it different from HIV?
AIDS (Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome) is a stage of HIV that eventually leads to death. It occurs after HIV has destroyed too many CD4 cells, which are cells in the immune system that activate other immune cells to fight off the infection. It usually takes around 10 years for a person with untreated HIV to develop AIDS, but ART treatment can slow the damage of HIV, preventing AIDS from developing for several decades.

HPV Viral

Most people have had it

Often none
HPV, also known as human papillomavirus, is the most common sexually-transmitted infection. It is usually harmless and goes away by itself, but certain types can lead to cancer or genital warts. Because it lacks symptoms much of the time and goes away, most people who have had it don’t know that they have. And most sexually-active people get HPV sometime in their lives. There are over 200 types of HPV, 40 of which infect the genitals (vulva, vagina, cervix, rectum, anus, penis, and scrotum). Two types cause genital warts (these are considered low-risk, as they don’t lead to serious health problems like cancer), but over a dozen (high-risk) cause cancer. The symptoms, such as genital warts and cervical cancer can be treated. For more info on genital warts and how they’re treated, check out the “Genital warts” row. HPV is a common cause of cervical cancer, but it can also just cause cancer in one’s vulva, vagina, penis, anus, mouth, and throat. This is why regular Pap/HPV tests are important. While there isn’t a cure, there are vaccines that can help protect against certain kinds of HPV, and usage of barrier methods such as dental dams and condoms can reduce risk as well (but they don’t offer perfect protection). It is spread through contact between skin-to-skin contact of genitals (vulva, vagina, cervix, penis, anus) of one person, and the genitals/mouth/throat of another person.

Molluscum contagiosum Viral
Little data on prevalence Small, hard bumps on skin
Molluscum contagiosum is a viral infection that causes small bumps on the face, neck, arms, stomach, legs, or genitals. Molluscum contagiosum is spread through close contact with someone who has it — during sex or from touching clothes, towels, or toys. Both adults and kids can get it, and it’s also possible to spread it to other parts of your body by touching or scratching the bumps. Molluscum contagiosum bumps usually go away by themselves, or a doctor can prescribe a medicated cream or remove them for you using different methods (freezing, draining, or laser removal). Molluscum contagiosum is spread through skin-to-skin contact, so using a condom does NOT prevent it; because of this, the best way to avoid it is to not have sex or skin-to-skin contact with someone who has it.

Pubic lice Parasitic 2% of adults Itching
Pubic lice (crabs) are small parasites that live on coarse hair (often near your genitals) and feed on your blood. They are spread during sexual contact or other kinds of close, personal contact; they can also be spread through an infected person’s clothes, towels, or bed. They do NOT spread through quick, casual touching, like hugs, and it’s extremely rare to get crabs from a toilet seat. Using a condom does NOT prevent a pubic lice infection. They cause lots of itching, but are usually pretty easy to get rid of, and treatment is often sold over-the-counter in gel, shampoo, liquid, or foam form.

Scabies Parasitic

Over 200 million people globally

 Itching, rashes, irritation
Scabies is a parasitic skin condition that is caused by scabies mites and spread through skin-to-skin contact. It causes rashes, irritation, and itching, but it can be cured with medicated creams or pills. In addition to being in close contact with someone who has scabies, you can also get scabies from sharing clothes, towels, or bedding with an infected person; using a condom does NOT prevent scabies transmission. It’s hard to get from quick, casual touching, or from toilet seats.

Syphilis Bacterial 133,945 U.S. cases (2020)

Very mild symptoms
Syphilis is a quite common bacterial STI that is easily curable with antibiotics, but will cause permanent damage (such as brain damage, paralysis, and blindness) gone untreated. It can infect one’s vagina, anus, penis, or scrotum, (and sometimes the lips or mouth), and it is spread through skin-to-skin contact when an uninfected person comes into contact with the sores of an infected person. However, these sores, and other symptoms (like rashes), are extremely difficult to notice. Even when they are noticed, they often get confused with other things like pimples and rashes. These symptoms may come and go with time, but the ONLY way to get rid of syphilis is to take the medicine for it. To help prevent syphilis, barrier methods like condoms and dental dams should be used, even if neither partner is showing symptoms. Unfortunately, syphilis can also be spread to a child during pregnancy (congenital syphilis), which can lead to many complications. For more details on that, read the first link below.

Trichomoniasis Parasitic

2.6 million cases in the U.S.

Usually none or vaginitis
Trichomoniasis (trich) is is a parasitic infection spread through sexual fluids (semen, pre-ejaculate, and vaginal fluids). There usually aren’t any symptoms, but there can include vaginitis (irritated vulva or vagina), urethral infection, pain when peeing, and smelly discharge. Trich is NOT spread during casual contact, and it is preventable by using condoms. Trich is the most common curable STI, and it can be cured using prescribed antibiotics.
